Artificial Intelligence (AI) — Overview
========================================

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to the simulation of human intelligence in
machines that are programmed to think and learn. The field was founded as an
academic discipline in 1956 and has experienced several waves of optimism,
followed by periods known as "AI winters."

Types of AI
------------
1. **Narrow AI (Weak AI)** — Systems designed for a specific task, such as voice
   assistants (Siri, Alexa), recommendation engines (Netflix, Spotify), and
   image recognition systems.

2. **General AI (Strong AI)** — A hypothetical system with the ability to
   understand, learn, and apply intelligence across any domain, similar to a
   human being. This has not yet been achieved.

3. **Superintelligent AI** — An AI that surpasses human intelligence in
   virtually every domain. This remains a theoretical concept discussed in
   futurism and philosophy.

Machine Learning
-----------------
Machine Learning (ML) is a subset of AI that enables systems to learn from data
without being explicitly programmed. Key approaches include:

- **Supervised Learning**: The model is trained on labelled data (e.g., spam
  detection).
- **Unsupervised Learning**: The model identifies hidden patterns in unlabelled
  data (e.g., customer segmentation).
- **Reinforcement Learning**: The model learns by receiving rewards or penalties
  for its actions (e.g., game-playing agents like AlphaGo).

Deep Learning
--------------
Deep Learning is a specialised form of ML that uses artificial neural networks
with many layers ("deep" networks). Notable architectures:

- **CNNs (Convolutional Neural Networks)** — Used for image and video analysis.
- **RNNs (Recurrent Neural Networks)** — Used for sequential data like text and
  time series.
- **Transformers** — The architecture behind GPT, BERT, and other large language
  models. Transformers use self-attention mechanisms to process input data in
  parallel, making them far more efficient than RNNs for many tasks.

Large Language Models (LLMs)
-----------------------------
LLMs such as GPT-4, LLaMA, and Gemini are trained on massive text corpora and
can generate human-like text. Key concepts:

- **Tokens**: The smallest units of text an LLM processes.
- **Context Window**: The maximum number of tokens an LLM can consider at once.
- **Fine-tuning**: Adapting a pre-trained model to a specific domain or task.
- **RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)**: A technique used to
  align LLM outputs with human preferences.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)
--------------------------------------
RAG is a technique that enhances LLM responses by retrieving relevant documents
from an external knowledge base before generating an answer. The typical RAG
pipeline consists of:

1. **Document Ingestion** — Loading and splitting documents into chunks.
2. **Embedding** — Converting text chunks into vector representations.
3. **Storage** — Storing embeddings in a vector database (e.g., ChromaDB, FAISS,
   Pinecone).
4. **Retrieval** — Finding the most relevant chunks for a given query using
   similarity search.
5. **Generation** — Feeding the retrieved context to an LLM to produce a
   grounded answer.

Benefits of RAG:
- Reduces hallucinations by grounding responses in real documents.
- Allows LLMs to access up-to-date or domain-specific information.
- More cost-effective than fine-tuning for many use cases.

Applications of AI
--------------------
- **Healthcare**: Disease diagnosis, drug discovery, medical imaging.
- **Finance**: Fraud detection, algorithmic trading, credit scoring.
- **Education**: Personalised tutoring, automated grading.
- **Transportation**: Self-driving cars, route optimisation.
- **Creative Arts**: Image generation (DALL·E, Midjourney), music composition.

Ethical Considerations
-----------------------
- **Bias**: AI systems can inherit and amplify biases present in training data.
- **Privacy**: Large-scale data collection raises significant privacy concerns.
- **Job Displacement**: Automation may displace certain types of work.
- **Safety**: Ensuring AI systems behave as intended, especially in high-stakes
  domains.
- **Transparency**: The "black box" nature of deep learning models makes
  interpretability a key challenge.
